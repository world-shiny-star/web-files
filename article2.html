Islamic sexisme

The discussion of women's rights in Islam is one of the most complex and debated topics in the modern world. To many outsiders—and indeed, to many within the Muslim community—certain Islamic laws and historical practices can appear profoundly unfair to women. Critics point to issues of inheritance, testimony, and social roles as evidence of systemic inequality. However, to understand this issue fully, one must navigate the intricate interplay between the original scriptural texts—the Quran and the Hadith (recorded sayings and actions of Prophet Muhammad)—and the cultural and patriarchal interpretations that have dominated Islamic jurisprudence for centuries. This article will explore the common areas of criticism, present the scriptural evidence often cited for both sides, and highlight the ongoing reinterpretation by Islamic feminists and scholars. 1. The Issue of Inheritance The Perception of Unfairness: A primary criticism is Islamic inheritance law, which often stipulates that a daughter receives half the share of a son. This is explicitly stated in the Quran: “Allah instructs you concerning your children: for the male, what is equal to the share of two females...” (Quran, Surah An-Nisa, 4:11) To a modern audience, this seems like a clear-cut case of gender-based discrimination. Context and Theological Rationale: Scholars argue that this verse cannot be read in isolation. When revealed in 7th-century Arabia, where female infants were often buried alive and women had no property rights, granting daughters a guaranteed share of inheritance was revolutionary. Furthermore, the financial responsibilities in a traditional Islamic framework are not equal. A man is solely financially responsible for providing for his wife, children, and sometimes other female relatives. His share is not solely his but is meant to support the entire family unit. A woman’s wealth, however, is entirely her own; she has no legal obligation to spend it on anyone, even herself. 2. The Issue of Testimony The Perception of Unfairness: Another frequently cited verse deals with financial contracts and testimony: “And bring to witness two witnesses from among your men. And if there are not two men [available], then a man and two women from those whom you accept as witnesses - so that if one of them [the women] errs, then the other can remind her.” (Quran, Surah Al-Baqarah, 2:282) This has been widely interpreted to mean that a woman's testimony is worth half that of a man's. Context and Theological Rationale: Many contemporary scholars emphasize that this verse is highly specific to financial contracts (dayn), considered the most complex legal matter of the time. The "error" mentioned is often explained not as intellectual inferiority, but as a lack of exposure. In 7th-century Arabia, women were generally not involved in complex commercial transactions and might have been less familiar with the details. The provision ensured the testimony's accuracy by involving two women who could corroborate the details together. It is crucial to note that in all other areas of law—especially those where women would have more direct knowledge, such as matters related to marriage, childbirth, and breastfeeding—the Quran and Hadith accept the testimony of a single woman as sufficient. The Prophet Muhammad himself settled a case based on the sole testimony of a woman (Bukhari). 3. The Issue of Polygyny The Perception of Unfairness: The permission for a man to marry up to four wives is often seen as the ultimate symbol of gender inequality. “...Marry those that please you of [other] women, two or three or four. But if you fear that you will not be just, then [marry only] one...” (Quran, Surah An-Nisa, 4:3) Context and Theological Rationale: This verse was revealed after the Battle of Uhud, where many Muslim men were killed, leaving widows and orphans without support. In that historical context, polygyny was a social welfare solution. The condition attached is immense: if you fear you cannot do justice, then only one. The Quran later underscores the near impossibility of this condition: “You will never be able to be just between wives, even if you should strive [to do so]...” (Quran, Surah An-Nisa, 4:129) Many modern reformers argue that these verses, read together, effectively restrict polygyny and that the overarching principle of justice makes monogamy the true Islamic ideal. 4. The Spiritual Equality Often lost in these legal debates is the Quran’s radical proclamation of spiritual and moral equality between men and women. “Surely the Muslim men and Muslim women, the believing men and believing women, the obedient men and obedient women, the truthful men and truthful women, the patient men and patient women, the humble men and humble women, the charitable men and charitable women, the fasting men and fasting women, the men who guard their chastity and the women who do so, and the men who remember Allah often and the women who do so - for them Allah has prepared forgiveness and a great reward.” (Quran, Surah Al-Ahzab, 33:35) This verse unequivocally states that spiritual reward is not gendered. Furthermore, the Quran holds both parties to the same ethical standards in social interactions, such as modesty: “Tell the believing men to lower their gaze and guard their modesty... And tell the believing women to lower their gaze and guard their modesty...” (Quran, Surah An-Nur, 24:30-31) Conclusion: Text vs. Interpretation The perceived "unfairness" to women in Islamic law often stems from a conflict between the text and its interpretation. The Quran introduced revolutionary rights for women in the 7th century—the right to inherit, own property, choose a spouse, and seek divorce. The Prophet Muhammad’s own life, as recorded in the Hadith, shows him consulting his wives, a businesswoman (Khadija) proposing to him, and him condemning the widespread practice of female infanticide. However, the subsequent interpretation and codification of Islamic law (Sharia) over the centuries were largely carried out by men in patriarchal societies. These interpretations often emphasized the restrictive aspects of the texts while minimizing their liberating and egalitarian spirit. Today, a growing movement of Muslim scholars and activists is engaging in ijtihad (independent reasoning) to re-examine these scriptures. They argue for a distinction between the immutable principles of the Quran—justice, mercy, compassion—and the mutable, time-bound laws that were applied in a specific historical context. For them, the quest is not to reject the faith, but to reclaim its foundational message of equality and justice for all, men and women alike. The challenge, and the hope, lies in separating divine injunction from human tradition.
